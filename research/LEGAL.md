## 1. Trademark issues

### 1.1 ISO standard names ("ISO 9001", "ISO 14001", etc.)

ISO ("International Organization for Standardization") protects both its short name "ISO" and its logo as registered trademarks. The standard *numbers* themselves (e.g. "9001", "14001", "27001") are not separately trademarked, but ISO's branding policy is strict about how the name is used.

Key rules from ISO's official guidance:

- The **ISO logo** may **only** be used by ISO, ISO members and ISO technical committees. ClickNComply must **never** display the ISO logo, even as decoration. Source: <https://www.iso.org/iso-name-and-logo.html>.
- **No product, including SaaS, may be described as "ISO-certified", "ISO 9001-certified" or "ISO-compliant".** Certification is awarded to a *management system* operated by an organisation, never to a product. Saying "ClickNComply is ISO 9001 certified software" would be a false statement and a misuse of ISO's name. Source: <https://simplyquality.org/publicity.pdf> (ISO publicity guidance).
- The full standard designation should be used in marketing copy, e.g. "ISO 9001:2015" rather than just "ISO 9001" where precision matters. Source: <https://www.iso.org/certification.html>.

**What ClickNComply *can* say (lower-risk wording):**

- "Templates that align with ISO 9001:2015 management-system requirements" ✅
- "Designed to help you build documentation for ISO 9001 / 14001 / 27001 certification" ✅
- "Generates a Quality Manual structured against the clauses of ISO 9001:2015" ✅
- "ISO 9001 templates" — borderline. ISO does not specifically prohibit this descriptive phrasing, but it should be paired elsewhere on the page with disclaimer wording such as "ClickNComply is not affiliated with, endorsed by or accredited by ISO." `[verify with solicitor — descriptive fair use likely OK, but lawyer can confirm safe harbour wording]`

**What ClickNComply *must not* say:**

- "ISO certified" / "ISO approved" / "ISO 9001 ready" (the last implies official endorsement) ❌
- Any use of the ISO logo ❌
- "Endorsed by ISO" / "Recommended by ISO" ❌

### 1.2 BS EN standards (e.g. "BS EN 1090")

BSI Group owns the copyright in **the text** of British Standards and BS EN standards in the UK (BSI also holds the UK copyright on European/international standards adopted by CEN/ISO). The **standard reference number** ("BS EN 1090") is descriptive and can be referenced factually, but **the text of the standard itself is copyrighted** and cannot be reproduced without a BSI licence. Source: <https://www.bsigroup.com/Documents/standards/guide-to-standards/BSI-Guide-to-standards-2-standard-structure-UK-EN.pdf>.

Implications for ClickNComply:

- ✅ Safe to reference standards by number ("templates for BS EN 1090-1 factory production control").
- ❌ Do **not** copy clause text, requirements lists, table contents or annexes from BS EN standards into ClickNComply outputs.
- ⚠️ AI-generated paraphrases of standard requirements are a grey area. Substantial reproduction of structure or close-paraphrased text may infringe copyright. Outputs should be **functionally aligned** with the standard but written in original language. `[verify with solicitor — substantiality is fact-specific]`
- For licensed reproduction, contact BSI Licensing at copyright@bsigroup.com. A commercial licence is realistically required if any clause text is reproduced.

### 1.3 CHAS

CHAS (Contractors Health and Safety Assessment Scheme) protects its logo and "Accredited Contractor" mark under copyright (CHAS Copyright). Only **paid-up CHAS-accredited contractors** may use the logo, and only on stationery and on promotional material accompanied by an objective explanation of the scheme. Source: <https://secure.chas.co.uk/ContractorPages/TermsAndConditions.html>.

Implications:

- ❌ ClickNComply cannot use the CHAS logo.
- ✅ ClickNComply can say "helps you prepare evidence for a CHAS assessment" or "templates structured around CHAS questionnaire categories".
- ❌ Do not say "CHAS approved software", "CHAS partner" or "CHAS recommended" unless a formal partner agreement is executed.
- The word "CHAS" itself is also a registered word mark in the UK; descriptive use to identify the scheme is generally permitted under nominative fair use, but trade-on-the-reputation language is not. `[verify with solicitor]`

### 1.4 Cyber Essentials

Cyber Essentials and Cyber Essentials Plus are owned by NCSC and exclusively administered for NCSC by IASME Consortium Ltd. The logo and certification mark may **only** be used by certified organisations, and only while their certificate is active (12 months from issue). Sources: <https://iasme.co.uk/cyber-essentials/terms-and-conditions/> and <https://www.ncsc.gov.uk/cyberessentials/overview>.

Implications:

- ❌ Cannot display the Cyber Essentials badge unless ClickNComply itself certifies (which is recommended anyway as a trust signal).
- ✅ Can describe templates as "structured around the Cyber Essentials five technical controls" — descriptive reference is permitted.
- ❌ Do not say "Cyber Essentials approved" or imply IASME / NCSC endorsement.

### 1.5 GDPR

"GDPR" is not trademarked. It is the popular name for Regulation (EU) 2016/679 and (in retained UK law) the UK GDPR. Free use in marketing is permitted, e.g. "GDPR-ready privacy notice templates" or "helps document your GDPR compliance". Avoid "GDPR-certified" because no single GDPR certification scheme exists in the UK or EU yet (Article 42 schemes are still emerging).

### 1.6 "Compliant" / "audit-ready" — generic terms

These are descriptive English, not trademarked. They are governed by the misleading-marketing rules in Section 2 below, not by trademark law.

### 1.7 Watermark wording — "Powered by ClickNComply"

Free-tier outputs carrying "Powered by ClickNComply":

- **Trademark / IP risk if a customer removes the watermark and presents the doc as their own work:** Under English law this could amount to **reverse passing off** (representing another's work as one's own) and a breach of the SaaS terms. Source: <https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Passing_off>. To rely on passing off, ClickNComply must establish goodwill — possible once the brand is known, harder pre-launch. The cleaner remedy is **contractual**: the Acceptable Use clause should expressly prohibit watermark removal on the free tier and reserve the right to suspend / claim damages.
- **Endorsement risk on the supplier's side (the customer's auditor seeing the watermark):** A "Powered by" line on a customer-facing compliance document could be perceived as ClickNComply endorsing the customer's compliance. Mitigate by adding small print on the watermarked page: "ClickNComply provides the document template only and does not endorse, audit or guarantee the content."

`[verify with solicitor]` — ensure the T&Cs grant ClickNComply a perpetual licence to reproduce customer logos / branding alongside the watermark on free-tier outputs, and that watermark removal is a defined material breach.

## 2. Marketing claims

### 2.1 The legal framework (UK)

UK marketing claims are policed by:

- **Consumer Protection from Unfair Trading Regulations 2008 (CPRs)** — civil/criminal penalties for misleading commercial practices.
- **Business Protection from Misleading Marketing Regulations 2008 (BPRs)** — B2B equivalent, governs comparative advertising.
- **CAP Code (non-broadcast)** enforced by the **ASA** — the principal voluntary regime, but ASA rulings are public and damaging. Source: <https://www.asa.org.uk/advice-online/misleading-advertising.html>.
- **Substantiation rule (CAP rule 3.7):** marketers must hold documentary evidence to prove all objective claims *before* publishing. Source: <https://www.asa.org.uk/advice-online/substantiation.html>.

In the EU: the **Unfair Commercial Practices Directive 2005/29/EC** mirrors these principles. In the US: **FTC Act §5** prohibits "unfair or deceptive acts or practices"; the FTC requires substantiation of all express and implied claims.

### 2.2 "Audit-ready" and "compliance-ready"

These are objective claims subject to substantiation. The ASA will assess them by their likely meaning to a reasonable consumer.

**Lower-risk wording:**

- "Generates the documents most auditors expect to see at an ISO 9001 stage-1 audit." (Specific, evidenced.)
- "Helps you prepare an audit-ready folder of policies, procedures and records." (Aspirational, qualified.)
- "From £9/month — replaces a £2,000 consultancy quote in many cases." (Comparative — see 2.4.)

**Higher-risk wording (avoid or qualify heavily):**

- "Guaranteed to pass your audit." ❌ — only true if ClickNComply guarantees the result, which it cannot.
- "100% compliant out of the box." ❌ — compliance depends on the customer's actual operations, not the document.
- "Audit-pass guarantee." ❌ unless backed by an actual contractual guarantee with refund mechanism.

**Mandatory qualifier** (recommended): every marketing page should carry, in legible type: *"ClickNComply provides documentation templates and AI-generated drafts. Audit outcomes depend on how your business operates the management system. ClickNComply does not guarantee certification."*

### 2.3 The line between helpful claims and misleading claims

Two ASA tests apply: (a) what does the average consumer take away as the main message? and (b) is that takeaway substantiated? If a small business owner reading the homepage would conclude "I will pass my ISO 9001 audit if I buy this for £9", and 30% of customers don't pass, that's a misleading omission.

Mitigations:

- Display **typical customer outcomes** with sample sizes if used as social proof ("of 142 customers surveyed, X% reported successful stage-1 audits").
- Prominently disclose the **role of the customer** in operating the management system.
- Avoid "all you need" / "everything required" claims unless literally true.

### 2.4 Comparative advertising — "cheaper than a £2k consultant"

UK position (BPRs 2008 + CAP rule 3.33): comparative ads are **legal** if they:

1. Compare goods/services meeting the same need;
2. Compare a material, relevant, verifiable and representative feature (price counts);
3. Are not misleading;
4. Do not denigrate the competitor;
5. Do not take unfair advantage of the competitor's reputation.

Source: <https://www.asa.org.uk/advice-online/comparisons-general.html>.

To say "**cheaper than a £2k consultant**" lawfully:

- ✅ Cite a verifiable basis ("based on Federation of Small Businesses 2025 survey of average ISO 9001 consultancy fees of £1,800–£2,400").
- ✅ Don't name a specific consultancy without holding evidence of *their* prices.
- ✅ State the comparison terms (what is included on each side, e.g. "consultant fees for documentation only, not implementation").
- ❌ Don't say "consultants are a rip-off" or similar denigration.

EU position: identical via Directive 2006/114/EC, retained in UK law.

US position (Lanham Act §43(a)): comparative ads are protected speech but plaintiffs (the named competitor) can sue for false advertising. Practical risk is low if claims are factually defensible and competitor not specifically named.

`[verify with solicitor]` — keep a one-page substantiation file for every comparative claim before launch.

### 2.5 Watermark wording IP / endorsement issue

See Section 1.7. Adding a small disclaimer below the watermark ("Document template provided by ClickNComply. Content quality and audit outcome remain the customer's responsibility.") substantially mitigates passing-off and endorsement risk.

## 3. Free trial / cancellation rules

### 3.1 Does the Consumer Rights Act 2015 (CRA) apply to B2B?

Largely **no**. CRA 2015 protects consumers, defined as natural persons acting outside their trade or business. A sole trader buying ClickNComply for their plumbing business is **not** a consumer. However:

- A **freelancer/sole-trader signing up in their personal capacity**, or a customer who later argues they signed up before incorporating, **could** be a consumer. The safe assumption for self-serve £9/mo SaaS is that **some sign-ups will be consumers**.
- The **Unfair Contract Terms Act 1977 (UCTA)** *does* apply to B2B contracts on standard terms, with a "reasonableness" test for liability limits.

Source: <https://www.gov.uk/government/publications/consumer-rights-act-2015>.

### 3.2 UK Consumer Contracts Regulations 2013 (CCRs)

Where a customer is a consumer and signs up online (distance contract):

- **14-day cancellation right** for services and digital content.
- For **services** (SaaS): the consumer can cancel within 14 days of signup. If they expressly request the service to start during the cooling-off period and acknowledge they will lose the cancellation right once the service is fully performed, the trader can charge for usage to date. Source: <https://www.legislation.gov.uk/uksi/2013/3134>.
- For **digital content downloads** (e.g. downloading a generated PDF): the consumer must give express consent to start the download during the cooling-off period and acknowledge loss of cancellation rights.

Practical implementation:

- Sign-up flow must show a tickbox: *"I want my subscription to start now and I understand I lose my right to cancel for any service already provided."*
- Refund policy must be clear and visible pre-checkout.

### 3.3 EU Consumer Rights Directive 2011/83/EU

Substantively identical to UK CCRs (CCRs implement this directive). 14-day cooling-off applies in every EU member state.

### 3.4 California — Automatic Renewal Law (amended 2024, effective 1 July 2025)

Replaces the older SB-313. Key requirements for any SaaS marketing to California residents:

- **Express affirmative consent** to the auto-renewal — separate tickbox.
- **Cancellation in the same medium** the consumer signed up in (online → online cancel button).
- **Annual reminder** for ≥1-year subscriptions.
- **Clear disclosure** of recurring charges before checkout.
- Up to **$2,500 per violation** in civil penalties.

Source: <https://oag.ca.gov/news/press-releases/attorney-general-bonta-issues-consumer-alert-california%E2%80%99s-automatic-renewal-law>.

### 3.5 US — FTC "Click-to-Cancel" Rule

The FTC's amended Negative Option Rule was **vacated by the Eighth Circuit on 8 July 2025** on procedural grounds, so it is **not currently in force federally**. Source: <https://www.consumerfinancemonitor.com/2025/07/23/eighth-circuit-voids-ftc-click-to-cancel-rule/>.

However:

- **State laws** (California, New York, Colorado, Vermont, Illinois and 20+ others) impose substantially similar requirements.
- The FTC may re-promulgate a corrected rule. `[verify before launch]`
- General FTC §5 deceptive-practice powers still apply to misleading sign-up flows.

**Practical position:** build the product to the strictest state standard (California) and you're compliant federally even if a new federal rule lands.

### 3.6 UK Digital Markets, Competition and Consumers Act 2024 (DMCC) — subscription regime

The DMCC's Part 4 Chapter 2 introduces a UK subscription-contracts regime. **Key facts:**

- Royal Assent: 24 May 2024.
- Subscription-trap reforms **not yet in force**. Latest government response (2 April 2026) targets **Spring 2027** for commencement of the subscription-contracts provisions.
- Source: <https://www.legislation.gov.uk/ukpga/2024/13/part/4/chapter/2> and <https://www.gov.uk/government/consultations/consultation-on-the-implementation-of-the-new-subscription-contracts-regime>.

What it will require (build to this now):

- Pre-contract information: total cost, renewal cadence, cancellation method.
- **Reminder notices** before each renewal — within a "reasonable period" before renewal for monthly subs; six months before for annual auto-renewals over 12 months.
- **14-day cooling-off after auto-renewal** (in addition to the initial 14 days).
- **Easy exit**: no penalty, same medium as sign-up.

Building to the DMCC standard now (even before commencement) future-proofs the product and is consistent with California ARL.

### 3.7 Australia / NZ

- **Australian Consumer Law** (Schedule 2 Competition and Consumer Act 2010): unfair-contract-terms regime now applies to standard-form B2C and small-business contracts (small business = <100 employees or <AU$10m turnover), with civil penalties up to AU$50m for unfair terms (since Nov 2023).
- **NZ Fair Trading Act 1986**: misleading conduct prohibited; very similar substantively to UK CPRs.
- ACCC has signalled active enforcement on subscription traps. Build to UK/California standard and Australia is largely covered.

## 4. GDPR for the platform itself

ClickNComply will hold customer compliance data — including potentially personal data of the customer's employees (training records, NCRs naming individuals, signed RAMS, etc.). The platform is therefore a **data processor** for those records, on behalf of customer organisations who are data controllers.

### 4.1 Processor obligations (UK GDPR Art. 28)

A written contract is mandatory between controller (customer) and processor (ClickNComply) — typically delivered as a **Data Processing Addendum (DPA)** linked from the main T&Cs. It must include:

- Subject matter, duration, nature and purpose of processing.
- Types of personal data and categories of data subjects.
- Controller's instructions; processor's confidentiality, security, sub-processor, breach-notification, deletion-on-termination, and audit obligations.

Source: <https://ico.org.uk/for-organisations/uk-gdpr-guidance-and-resources/accountability-and-governance/contracts-and-liabilities-between-controllers-and-processors-multi/what-needs-to-be-included-in-the-contract/>.

**When is a signed DPA needed?**

- Always required by law where a controller-processor relationship exists.
- For self-serve SaaS, the standard practice is a **click-through DPA** built into the sign-up T&Cs, which is sufficient for UK/EU GDPR purposes.
- Some larger customers will demand a counter-signed DPA with their own paper. Build a templated DPA you can send out without bespoke negotiation.

### 4.2 Privacy policy minimum content (UK GDPR Articles 13–14)

Must state: identity and contact of the controller; DPO contact (if appointed); purposes and lawful basis for each processing activity; legitimate interests relied on; recipients/categories of recipients; international transfers and the safeguard used; retention periods; data-subject rights (access, rectification, erasure, restriction, portability, objection, withdraw consent); right to complain to the ICO; whether data is required for contract; existence of automated decision-making.

Source: <https://ico.org.uk/for-organisations/uk-gdpr-guidance-and-resources/individual-rights/the-right-to-be-informed/what-privacy-information-should-we-provide/>.

Note: the **Data (Use and Access) Act 2025** received Royal Assent on 19 June 2025 and amends parts of the UK GDPR/DPA 2018. ICO guidance is still being updated. `[verify with solicitor before publishing privacy policy]`.

### 4.3 Sub-processor disclosure (Art. 28(2)–(4))

Required sub-processors to declare publicly on the website (recommended a `/sub-processors` page):

| Sub-processor | Service | Region |
|---|---|---|
| Anthropic PBC | LLM API (document generation) | US |
| Supabase Inc. | Database, auth, storage | EU (Frankfurt) — confirm region selected |
| Vercel Inc. | Hosting / edge compute | US (and global edge) |
| Stripe Inc. / Stripe Payments UK Ltd | Payments | US/UK |
| Resend | Transactional email | US |
| (any analytics / error monitoring — e.g. Sentry, PostHog, Plausible) | | |

The DPA must allow ClickNComply to engage sub-processors with **prior general authorisation** and a duty to notify customers of changes. ICO accepts general authorisation provided customers can object.

### 4.4 International data transfers

Most listed sub-processors are US-based. Mechanisms available:

- **EU-US Data Privacy Framework (DPF)**: adequacy decision adopted July 2023 — Anthropic, Vercel, Stripe and Resend can be on the DPF list. **Verify each is currently certified** at <https://www.dataprivacyframework.gov/list>.
- **UK extension to the DPF**: separate adequacy. Verify each sub-processor is certified for the **UK extension** specifically.
- **UK IDTA** or **EU SCCs + UK IDTA Addendum**: fallback if DPF is not in place. ICO mandates these for non-adequate transfers from 21 March 2024. Source: <https://ico.org.uk/for-organisations/uk-gdpr-guidance-and-resources/international-transfers/international-data-transfer-agreement-and-guidance/>.

**Practical position:** rely on DPF for US sub-processors (with UK extension), and have IDTA / SCCs as the contractual fallback. State this clearly in the privacy policy.

A **Transfer Risk Assessment (TRA)** should be performed and documented for each non-DPF transfer. ICO has a free TRA tool: <https://ico.org.uk/for-organisations/uk-gdpr-guidance-and-resources/international-transfers/international-transfers-a-guide/>.

### 4.5 ICO guidance for compliance SaaS

No compliance-SaaS-specific ICO guidance exists. The general SaaS / cloud guidance applies:

- Cloud computing checklist: <https://ico.org.uk/for-organisations/uk-gdpr-guidance-and-resources/security/a-guide-to-data-security/>.
- Detailed processor contract guidance (cited above).
- Pay attention to **special category data** if customers upload health-related NCRs (occupational health) or biometric data.

## 5. Liability

### 5.1 If a customer fails an audit, can they sue ClickNComply?

Theoretical claims:

- **Breach of contract** (services not fit for purpose) — defended by clear T&Cs that ClickNComply provides templates and AI drafting tools, not certification.
- **Misrepresentation Act 1967** — risk only if marketing claims overpromise (see Section 2).
- **Negligent misstatement** (Hedley Byrne v Heller) — risk if ClickNComply assumes responsibility for advice. Mitigated by clearly framing outputs as "draft documentation requiring customer review", **not** "advice".
- **Consumer Rights Act 2015** s.49 (services performed with reasonable care and skill) — applies to consumer customers, cannot be excluded.

**Defensive contract architecture:**

- Define ClickNComply's role as **"a software platform that generates documentation drafts"** — not a consultancy, not a certification body.
- Customer covenants: "Customer is solely responsible for reviewing, customising, implementing and operating any management system."
- Express disclaimer: AI outputs may contain errors, and require customer review before use.
- Carve out fitness-for-purpose warranties to the maximum extent permitted by law.

### 5.2 Limitation of liability — what's enforceable?

UK position (UCTA 1977):

- **Cannot exclude/limit:** death or personal injury caused by negligence; fraud; statutory rights (CRA 2015 ss.49, 51, 52 for consumers) to the extent excluded by the Act.
- **B2B reasonableness test:** caps must be "reasonable" in the circumstances. £9/mo SaaS to SMEs — a cap of "the greater of £100 and 12 months' fees paid" is industry-standard and likely reasonable.
- **Indirect / consequential / loss of profits:** can be excluded in B2B with clear wording.

Source: <https://www.legislation.gov.uk/ukpga/1977/50>.

EU position: similar, member-state-specific. Some jurisdictions (Germany, France) have stricter consumer-protection limits.

US position: caps are generally enforceable in B2B if conspicuous, but consumer arbitration / class-action waivers face state-by-state scrutiny (esp. California).

**Recommended liability cap clause** `[verify with solicitor]`:

> "To the fullest extent permitted by law, ClickNComply's total aggregate liability arising out of or in connection with this Agreement shall not exceed the greater of (a) £100, or (b) the fees paid by the Customer in the 12 months preceding the claim. Neither party shall be liable for indirect, special, incidental, consequential, exemplary or punitive damages, or for any loss of profit, revenue, business opportunity, goodwill or anticipated savings. Nothing in this clause limits liability for death or personal injury caused by negligence, fraud or fraudulent misrepresentation, or any other liability that cannot be excluded by law."

### 5.3 Case law on AI-generated compliance docs

There is **no binding UK case law** specifically on AI-generated compliance documentation as of April 2026. Useful adjacent authorities:

- *Moffatt v Air Canada* (2024, Canada — small-claims) — airline held responsible for an AI chatbot's incorrect refund advice. Principle: companies are liable for the outputs of AI tools they deploy. <https://www.canlii.org/en/bc/bccrt/doc/2024/2024bccrt149/2024bccrt149.html>.
- US legal-tech: *Mata v Avianca* (2023) — sanctions against lawyers who filed ChatGPT-fabricated case citations. Principle: AI hallucinations don't excuse the human user.

These confirm: ClickNComply is responsible for outputs the platform produces, and customers are responsible for what they file/use. T&Cs should reinforce both points.

### 5.4 Are we providing "legal advice" or "regulated consultancy"?

Risk **only if** ClickNComply:

- Holds itself out as a law firm or a consultancy ("our legal team", "consultancy service").
- Performs **reserved legal activities** (litigation, advocacy, conveyancing, probate, oaths, certain notarial acts) — N/A.

Source: <https://www.lawsociety.org.uk/Topics/AI-and-lawtech/Whats-changing/AI-lawtech-policy>.

The SRA has authorised the first AI-driven law firm (Garfield AI, May 2025) but only after a regulated-firm authorisation process. ClickNComply is **not** a regulated firm and should never present as one.

**Mitigation:** every output footer and the T&Cs should state: *"ClickNComply is a software platform. It does not provide legal, regulatory or accountancy advice. Where you require advice, consult an appropriately qualified professional."*

### 5.5 Professional Indemnity / Tech E&O insurance

Recommended cover for a self-serve SaaS at this stage:

- **Technology E&O / professional indemnity:** £2m limit (entry-level), rising to £5m once enterprise customers appear.
- **Cyber liability:** £1m–£2m limit (covers breach response, ICO fines defence, ransomware).
- **Public liability:** £1m (standard, even office-only).
- **D&O:** advisable post-Series A.

Source: <https://simplify.stream/professional-indemnity-saas-uk/>.

Specialist SaaS / tech brokers: Hiscox, Markel, Beazley, Hamilton Fraser, Get Indemnity. Premiums for early-stage SaaS at <£500k revenue typically £800–£2,000/yr for £2m PI + £1m cyber. `[verify with broker]`

## 6. AI-specific considerations

### 6.1 Anthropic Usage Policy

Anthropic's Usage Policy (last updated 15 September 2025) governs all use of the Claude API. Key constraints relevant to ClickNComply:

- **No "high-risk" consumer-facing decisions without human oversight.** Compliance documentation generation is on the borderline of "high-risk" because business audits / certification depend on it. ClickNComply **must** retain human-review by the customer before any document is filed externally.
- **No legal-advice automation without disclosure.** ClickNComply outputs must clearly state they are drafts requiring customer review.
- **No use that is "deceptive or disruptive to democratic processes"** — N/A.
- **API logs:** retained 7 days (down from 30) and not used for model training. Source: <https://www.anthropic.com/news/usage-policy-update> and <https://support.claude.com/en/articles/8241216-i-m-planning-to-launch-a-product-using-the-claude-api-what-steps-should-i-take-to-ensure-i-m-not-violating-anthropic-s-usage-policy>.

ClickNComply must also flow Anthropic's user-facing acceptable-use restrictions through to its own end users via the AUP.

### 6.2 EU AI Act 2024 — high-risk classification?

The AI Act entered into force on 1 August 2024; full applicability 2 August 2026. Source: <https://artificialintelligenceact.eu/high-level-summary/>.

ClickNComply's likely classification:

- **General-purpose AI deployer** (uses Anthropic Claude). Most obligations (transparency, AUP propagation) sit with the *provider* (Anthropic), not the deployer.
- **Annex III high-risk uses** include: HR/employment decisions, education access, credit, law-enforcement, biometric ID. **Compliance documentation generation is not currently in Annex III.** The product is therefore **most likely "limited-risk"** (Article 50 transparency obligations only). `[verify with solicitor — particularly if HR features expand]`.
- **Article 50 transparency obligations** (apply from August 2026):
  - Inform users they are interacting with an AI system.
  - Mark AI-generated text in machine-readable form (e.g. metadata) where it is published as informing the public on matters of public interest. Customer-internal compliance docs may fall outside this trigger, but the safe approach is to mark all AI outputs.
  - Source: <https://artificialintelligenceact.eu/article/50/>.

### 6.3 Output ownership — who owns the AI-generated docs?

UK position:

- **Copyright:** under s.9(3) CDPA 1988, computer-generated works (no human author) are owned by "the person by whom the arrangements necessary for the creation of the work are undertaken" — typically ClickNComply, as the operator. *However*, the UK government has consulted on whether AI outputs should remain copyrightable; the position is uncertain post-2026.
- **Practical position:** the T&Cs should grant the customer a **perpetual, worldwide, royalty-free licence** to use the outputs for their own internal compliance purposes. ClickNComply retains underlying IP in templates, prompts and platform.

US position: outputs without human authorship are **not protectable by copyright** (US Copyright Office, March 2023; *Thaler v Perlmutter*, 2025). Customers may not be able to assert copyright in their generated docs. `[verify with US counsel]`.

### 6.4 AI disclosure requirements

Required (or strongly recommended):

- **In-app:** label outputs visibly as "AI-generated draft — review before use".
- **Marketing pages:** state that documents are AI-generated, not consultancy-produced.
- **Privacy policy:** disclose Anthropic as a sub-processor and state that customer prompts are sent to Anthropic.
- **For EU customers (from August 2026):** machine-readable marking of generated text per AI Act Art. 50.

## 7. Specific operational requirements

### 7.1 Privacy policy

Minimum content (UK GDPR + UK Data (Use and Access) Act 2025 + CCPA): identity, contact, DPO (if any), purposes, lawful bases, data categories, recipients/sub-processors, transfers + safeguards, retention, rights, complaints, children's data position, cookies cross-link, automated decision-making, US-residents notice (CCPA "Do Not Sell or Share"), updates date.

For CCPA (California Consumer Privacy Act): if you sell to California residents and meet thresholds (>$25m revenue, >100k consumers, >50% revenue from selling personal info), full CCPA applies. **Below thresholds you are not subject to CCPA**, but a "Do Not Sell or Share" link and a CCPA notice section is best practice. `[verify with solicitor when US revenue >$25m]`.

### 7.2 Terms of Service essentials (£9 self-serve SaaS)

Must-have clauses:

1. Definitions and contract formation (clickwrap acceptance).
2. Service description and disclaimers (no warranty of audit success).
3. Subscription, billing, auto-renewal, cancellation (DMCC-ready).
4. Acceptable Use Policy reference.
5. Customer data ownership; ClickNComply IP ownership; licence grant.
6. Confidentiality.
7. Data protection / DPA incorporation.
8. Liability cap, exclusions, indemnities.
9. Suspension and termination.
10. Governing law and jurisdiction (England & Wales recommended; consider arbitration carve-out for US customers).
11. Variation, severability, entire agreement, notices.
12. Anti-bribery, sanctions, export control basic compliance.

### 7.3 Cookies (PECR + ePrivacy)

UK PECR Reg 6 + ICO 2025 guidance: **prior consent** required for any non-strictly-necessary storage/access tech (cookies, pixels, fingerprinting, web storage). Pre-ticked boxes invalid. "Reject all" must be as easy as "Accept all". Source: <https://ico.org.uk/for-organisations/direct-marketing-and-privacy-and-electronic-communications/guidance-on-the-use-of-storage-and-access-technologies/>.

Strictly-necessary only (no consent needed): session cookies, security/CSRF tokens, load-balancing, basic auth.

Consent-required: analytics (PostHog, GA4, Plausible without server-side anonymisation), marketing pixels, A/B testing.

**Implementation:** use a CMP (Cookiebot, Iubenda, OneTrust free tier, or open-source Klaro). PECR maximum fine post Data (Use and Access) Act 2025: £17.5m or 4% global turnover.

### 7.4 Refund / cancellation policy — best practice

For £9/mo self-serve:

- **No-quibble cancellation any time** — avoids friction and satisfies all jurisdictions.
- **Pro-rata refund optional** but not required (most SaaS does not pro-rata).
- **14-day full refund** for first-time consumer customers (CCRs requirement; extend to all customers as goodwill).
- One-click cancel from in-app billing page (DMCC + California ARL ready).
- Confirmation email after cancellation.

### 7.5 Acceptable Use Policy

Cover: no illegal use; no infringing IP; no using outputs to deceive auditors / submit to authorities without review; no automated scraping of platform; no removing watermarks on free tier; flow-through of Anthropic restrictions; suspension and termination grounds.

### 7.6 VAT (UK + EU)

UK threshold: must register for VAT once UK taxable turnover exceeds **£90,000** (rate from 1 April 2024). Below threshold: registration optional. Source: <https://www.gov.uk/vat-registration>.

**Important:** for **B2C digital services to EU consumers**, there is **no threshold** — VAT must be charged from the first sale at the consumer's local rate.

EU mechanism (post-Brexit): UK businesses cannot use UK MOSS; must register for either:

- **EU OSS Non-Union scheme** (single registration in one EU member state, covers all EU B2C sales), or
- VAT in each member state directly.

For **B2B** sales to EU businesses (with valid VAT number), reverse charge applies — invoice with no VAT, customer accounts for it locally.

Source: <https://stripe.com/guides/introduction-to-eu-vat-and-european-vat-oss>.

### 7.7 Stripe Tax — does it handle EU VAT MOSS?

Yes, **Stripe Tax** automatically:

- Calculates correct VAT rate by customer location / B2B status.
- Validates EU VAT numbers for B2B reverse-charge.
- Generates compliant invoices.
- Produces the OSS return data — but **does not file** the OSS return for you. You still need to register with HMRC's non-Union OSS gateway in an EU member state and submit the quarterly return. Stripe Tax export gives you the figures.

US sales tax: Stripe Tax handles state economic nexus calculation and registration via their partner (TaxJar / Anrok / Avalara recommended). Critical thresholds: most US states ~$100k or 200 transactions per state per year.

Source: <https://docs.stripe.com/tax/supported-countries/european-union>.

### 7.8 Other operational items

- **Companies House:** ClickNComply Ltd must show company number and registered office on the website footer (Companies Act 2006 s.82).
- **Modern Slavery / Tax Strategy / s172 statements:** thresholds far above current scale — N/A until ~£36m revenue.
- **Accessibility:** WCAG 2.2 AA recommended (legally required for public-sector customers).
- **Children's data:** clearly state in T&Cs the service is for B2B users 18+. No COPPA / Age-Appropriate-Design-Code concerns at this scale.

## Summary — six critical legal gotchas before launch

1. **Trademark wording on the homepage.** Replace any phrasing implying ISO/CHAS/Cyber Essentials endorsement with descriptive wording ("templates aligned with ISO 9001:2015"). Add a single-line disclaimer in the footer disowning affiliation. Never display the ISO logo; never say "ISO certified".
2. **Subscription / cancel architecture.** Build now to the **strictest** combination of California ARL (effective 1 July 2025) + UK DMCC subscription regime (likely Spring 2027): one-click cancel in same medium, separate auto-renew tickbox at sign-up, pre-renewal reminders, 14-day cooling-off after auto-renewal.
3. **DPA + sub-processor list live before paid launch.** Click-through DPA inside T&Cs and a public `/sub-processors` page listing Anthropic, Supabase, Vercel, Stripe, Resend with regions and transfer mechanisms. Confirm each is on the **EU-US DPF and UK extension** today; if not, fall back to UK IDTA.
4. **Liability ceiling and "not advice" framing.** Liability cap clause (greater of £100 or 12 months' fees), indirect-loss exclusion, and a clear product description: ClickNComply is **a software platform that generates documentation drafts requiring customer review**. Never "advisor", "consultant" or "guarantor of audit success". Get £2m Tech E&O insurance in place pre-launch.
5. **AI disclosures.** Visible "AI-generated draft" labelling in-app and on every output. Privacy-policy disclosure of Anthropic as sub-processor. Plan for EU AI Act Article 50 transparency obligations from August 2026 (machine-readable marking).
6. **VAT registration before going live in EU.** Either register for VAT in the UK (mandatory once you cross £90k UK turnover) and EU OSS Non-Union scheme (mandatory from the first EU B2C sale of digital services). Configure Stripe Tax day one — but remember it doesn't file the OSS return for you.
